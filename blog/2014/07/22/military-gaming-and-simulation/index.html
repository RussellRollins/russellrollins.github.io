Military Gaming and Simulation

This past semester I took a class in military simulation and gaming. The course focused on developing a computer model of a specified military confrontation, and using that simulation to answer a research question about the battle. This is a blog post that I wrote while finishing the course, and reflecting on the details of the project.

The Project

Like many people from the South I have a deep interest in the American Civil War. I have family members who fought on both sides of the Civil War, and it's the only war really fought in and around my home state of Florida. Naturally, I was excited when our group choose to model The Battle of Antietam, a battle fought in Maryland early in the war. There is an interesting moment in the battle where a pivotal bridge crossing was flubbed. General Burnside (yes, of sidesburns fame) has become infamous for this failure, and preventing a decisive Union Victory. We're hoping to answer a general question about the corssing of obstacles under fire, and use the insights we develop to either confirm this belief or clearn Burnsides's (admittedly sily) name.

The Team

Our group is made up of an Internation Affairs (INTA) Graduate Student, an INTA Undergraduate, and Industrial Engineering (IE) Grad Student, an IE Undergraduate, and myself (CMPE). I love working with non-technical people. My last job was at a storytelling startup in Atlanta, Umenta. Due to the nature of Umenta's work, there was a full time interviewer on staff (a former attorney) and a rotating cast of interns (journalists). Since I left Umenta, I've been working heads down on finishing my degree in Computer Engineering. This means I mostly spend time working on engineering with engineers. I miss coding full time, and I miss working with a wider cast of people. It's easy to get tunnel vision when you only talk and spend time with people similar to yourself. It's humbling to work with someone who is smarter than you, but in a totally different way. It's always a good way to open your eyes to different ways of thinking. The two IE team members have some coding abilities (Python and R), but they certaily aren't coders by profession. There were some blank states when I suggested we use Git for our project. It's important to remember not to define jargon in terms of jargon, "open source version control" was a thoroughly unhelpful answer. There's no better way to consolidate understanding of a subject than explaining it to someone, and I'm sure I'll learn a lot of new coding skills working with this group. I think we have a lot of talkent, and I'm excited to see what we produce.

The Tool

For our model, We're using NetLogo, a multi-agent modeling environment. The tool is powerful, flexible, and fairly easy to use. NetLogo is open source and source can be found on GitHub. Interestingly, it's coded in Scala, and when I get some downtime I'd like to spend some time with the source code. NetLogo is fully programmable in it's own scripting language, which uses an Agent-based modeling technique. The Agent-based programming differs enough from procedural or object oriented programming to throw me for a bit of a loop.

The Issues

The docs for Netlogo aren't half bad, and it's not impossible to get your bearings with this lanaguage. However, our class has offered limited guidance on how to write code for it, and there aren't a wealth of good tutorials on the internet. The simple tutorial on NetLogo's site is really only a toy example, and leaves you hanging on a lot of important topics in the language. StackOverflow is largely silent on the topic. I hope it doesn't become an issue. It's a good example of how using an unusual tool can create extra work.
