Penetration Testing a Combination Lock

My new job is at a fairly locked-down facility, and that's had me thinking about physical security lately. Monday morning I was in a rush at the gym and entered the combination into my padlock incorrectly. I didn't miss the number by a huge amount, but it certainly wasn't right. The lock opened anyway. It's been a long time since I used combination locks, so I didn't really remember this fudge factor. Obviously this introduces a lot of insecurity into my lock. We just went from a solution space of 1 to n^3, where n is the number of permissible numbers at each turn.

I've also been taking a class in Web Attack and Defense at work, and I was interested in whether some of those techniques might apply to this lock problem, and what I could learn about physical and digital security. There are many ways to penetration test a website, but the course recommends the following pattern:

Map -> Analyze -> Investigate -> Exploit

I'm going to try and see how this technique works on my lock.

Map

Okay, this part is easy. Examine the intended behaviors of the application, and check how it responds to inputs. We're looking for error messages, data entry points, and any obvious flaws. I've only got one way to input data to the lock and that's by turning it. I've got two other commands I can execute. Unlock is performed by pulling on the lock-y thing (I, uh, really don’t know much about locks). Reset is performed by turning the lock clockwise three times. In summary, one data entry method, and two commands. Let's test some data.

First up, my actual combination. I'm going to go ahead and tell you, it's 02-16-21. If someone on the Internet actually tracks me down at the gym, I've got bigger concerns than my stolen pants. Anyway, I input this combination, and do an unlock. The lock opens. Good.

Next, a very wrong combination, 01-01-01. I input, and perform an unlock. Error, incorrect combination. Lock doesn't open.

How about an almost right combination, 02-16-22. Input and unlock. Lock opens.

Let's test reset. I will input the correct first two numbers, reset, then put in the third correct number and unlock. Error, incorrect combination (or incomplete combination I guess). Lock doesn't open.

This basic exploration tells us a couple things. The lock will open to correct or nearly correct combinations. It doesn't open for a very wrong combination. Reset and unlock work as expected.

Analyze

What do I know about locks? Not a whole lot. I learned to operate a combination lock in junior high, but I don't know how it works. I use keyed locks more often, and I have a vague understanding of how they work (something to do with tumblers, I saw a .gif about how lockpicking works once).

What have I learned? Well, for one, it seems like a locksmith is going to be able to Italian Job my shoes no matter what I do. Keeping valuables out of the locker is probably the smartest move. I also have a theory about why the tolerance exists on my lock. The "notches" are wider than the "fence". Leaving a decent room for error. Oh, and the lock-y thing from before is called a “shackle”.

What else can I find out? The gym has some information. They advise buying a lock resistant to being "shimmed". What does that mean? Oh. Good. You can probably open my locker with a soda can.

Lifehacker Shim A Lock

They also say they cut locks off overnight. So I also know the locks can be brute forced (in the most literal sense). Bolt cutters or a hacksaw would do the job.

What about this specific lock? I bought it at CVS. It was cheap. The cheapest option. So I'm assuming it's got worse tolerances, lower build quality, and fewer defense mechanisms than a more expensive lock. So maybe I can't generalize any exploits I create to other pricier locks.

Finally, let's think about some social engineering attacks. A gym official cuts locks off overnight. Probably anyone with bolt cutters and the right Polo shirt could pull that attack off. I'm also going to make an assertion that locker combos aren't normally distributed. Mine is all decently separated numbers. If, say, my mom, bought a lock and the combination was 01-01-01, I don't think she would consider that combination theory says that's just one of the combinations the lock should have. I think she'd say it was a shitty lock.

Overall, like most computer systems, it looks like any attacker with unrestricted physical access is going to defeat my locker. Fortunately, like many lockers, mine is in a locker room with decent traffic. Hopefully, a good Samaritan would question you if you were hacksawing my lock or shoving a cut-up can into it. Hopefully.

Investigate

I'm jumping from this model to the scientific method for a bit because I feel pretty good about my angle of attack. I've done some research, but I'd like to know something else. What's the wrongest number I can use and get an open lock?

A quick check indicates that incredibly, the second number can be off by as much as 14(!) if you you overshoot. The other two are more resistant, and the second number is more resistant to undershoot. I think this throws a wrench in my original theory about wide gates. The issue is neither symmetric nor consistent. In fact the overshoot issue screams intentional design choice. It makes it easy to open the lock really fast.

Hypothesis: the level of error in the accepted numbers will allow me to reduce the combination space for the lock to less than 800 combinations.

Experiment: I tested offsets of [-2,-1,0,1,2] for each of the three inputs. That’s 125 combinations.

I also made a secondary observation during testing. If you jiggle the shackle, it will sometimes open when it wouldn’t normally have. I didn’t do this for my test because II discovered it halfway through, and it would have skewed the data. Still, it’s a valuable piece of knowledge.

Conclusion: Well, my hypothesis is wrong. That crazy tolerance in the second number isn’t always there. It only works when the other two numbers are perfectly correct. I also learned that the first number is the most resistant to tampering. The lock never opens if the first number is off by more than one. That’s a reasonable design decision, if it’s intentional. Someone who knows the first number probably knows all three. Finally, I learned that if the offset has an absolute value of one or less, you can offset all three positions. This means that each attempt actually eliminates three possible combinations. This reduces the test space to 2744.

Application: I can build a device that automates this process to a great enough degree that a new lock with an unknown combination can be brute force cracked in under an hour. More about this in the next section.

Exploit

Now that I that I know all this, I’m going to design some exploits. The first is to test every third number, and brute force the combination. The tolerance in the design means I don't have to get the right combination. I just need to get an almost right combination. If me and this lock were playing battleship, I just stopped looking for the patrol boat and started looking for the carrier. This reduces the set of necessary brute force guesses from 64000 to 2744. Things don't look good for the lock. Still, it takes me about 15 seconds to do each test, this will take a long time. Long enough that I’m not going to do it, I’m just confident I could.

I tried the shim technique. I mean, how many lockpicking kits come with a free sugar sweetened beverage? It (like many things in life) turned out to be harder than the guy on YouTube made it look. All I ended up with was some soda and cut fingers. Score one for the lock.

I also thought about hacksawing or bolt cutting the lock, just to see how hard it would be. But I’ve only got the one lock, and I still need it. I’m confident it wouldn’t be too difficult to pull this off.

Overall, what can I take away from this? Don't expect your attacker to do things the hard way. This "exploit" on the lock is pointless, because 30 seconds with the shim (if you’re better at it than me) or 10 seconds with the bolt cutters works a whole lot better. Don't allow unrestricted physical access to any security system. This lock doesn't keep thieves out. It just makes thieves easy to spot. A good security system is one that makes bad actors easy to spot. One guy sitting in the locker trying thousands of lock combinations or working a lockpick would raise suspicions. I also saw that security is only as strong as the weakest link. This exploit makes a strong link weaker, but it would still be easier to go another way.

Conclusion

I think I could build a machine that automates the cracking process. I also didn’t apply the theory that the lock combination is probably spread out. I’d also like to try and write a python script that prioritizes the “good” combinations. This was a fun exploration, but I don't know if I'll ever have much faith in combination locks again.

Military Gaming and Simulation

This past semester I took a class in military simulation and gaming. The course focused on developing a computer model of a specified military confrontation, and using that simulation to answer a research question about the battle. This is a blog post that I wrote while finishing the course, and reflecting on the details of the project.

The Project

Like many people from the South I have a deep interest in the American Civil War. I have family members who fought on both sides of the Civil War, and it's the only war really fought in and around my home state of Florida. Naturally, I was excited when our group choose to model The Battle of Antietam, a battle fought in Maryland early in the war. There is an interesting moment in the battle where a pivotal bridge crossing was flubbed. General Burnside (yes, of sidesburns fame) has become infamous for this failure, and preventing a decisive Union Victory. We're hoping to answer a general question about the corssing of obstacles under fire, and use the insights we develop to either confirm this belief or clearn Burnsides's (admittedly sily) name.

The Team

Our group is made up of an Internation Affairs (INTA) Graduate Student, an INTA Undergraduate, and Industrial Engineering (IE) Grad Student, an IE Undergraduate, and myself (CMPE). I love working with non-technical people. My last job was at a storytelling startup in Atlanta, Umenta. Due to the nature of Umenta's work, there was a full time interviewer on staff (a former attorney) and a rotating cast of interns (journalists). Since I left Umenta, I've been working heads down on finishing my degree in Computer Engineering. This means I mostly spend time working on engineering with engineers. I miss coding full time, and I miss working with a wider cast of people. It's easy to get tunnel vision when you only talk and spend time with people similar to yourself. It's humbling to work with someone who is smarter than you, but in a totally different way. It's always a good way to open your eyes to different ways of thinking. The two IE team members have some coding abilities (Python and R), but they certaily aren't coders by profession. There were some blank states when I suggested we use Git for our project. It's important to remember not to define jargon in terms of jargon, "open source version control" was a thoroughly unhelpful answer. There's no better way to consolidate understanding of a subject than explaining it to someone, and I'm sure I'll learn a lot of new coding skills working with this group. I think we have a lot of talkent, and I'm excited to see what we produce.

The Tool

For our model, We're using NetLogo, a multi-agent modeling environment. The tool is powerful, flexible, and fairly easy to use. NetLogo is open source and source can be found on GitHub. Interestingly, it's coded in Scala, and when I get some downtime I'd like to spend some time with the source code. NetLogo is fully programmable in it's own scripting language, which uses an Agent-based modeling technique. The Agent-based programming differs enough from procedural or object oriented programming to throw me for a bit of a loop.

The Issues

The docs for Netlogo aren't half bad, and it's not impossible to get your bearings with this lanaguage. However, our class has offered limited guidance on how to write code for it, and there aren't a wealth of good tutorials on the internet. The simple tutorial on NetLogo's site is really only a toy example, and leaves you hanging on a lot of important topics in the language. StackOverflow is largely silent on the topic. I hope it doesn't become an issue. It's a good example of how using an unusual tool can create extra work.
